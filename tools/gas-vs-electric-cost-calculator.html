Comparing Gas and Electric Heating Costs

The choice between gas and electric heating has significant long-term financial implications for homeowners. Natural gas heating typically offers lower operating costs in areas where gas infrastructure is available, but electric heat pump technology has advanced to the point where it is competitive with gas in many climates, particularly when the dual benefits of heating and cooling are considered. This calculator provides a direct cost comparison between fuel types based on your local utility rates, helping you make an informed decision that aligns with your budget and comfort priorities.

When evaluating fuel options, consider that natural gas prices are subject to market fluctuations driven by supply, demand, and geopolitical factors, while electricity prices tend to be more stable over time. Heat pumps also offer the advantage of being 250-400% efficient, meaning they deliver 2.5 to 4 times more heat energy than the electrical energy they consume. This efficiency advantage can offset higher electricity prices in many regions. Additionally, homes with heat pumps do not require separate air conditioning systems, potentially reducing the total equipment investment by $3,000-$6,000 compared to installing both a gas furnace and a central AC unit.

Gas vs Electric Heating: The Basics

Comparing gas and electric heating costs requires understanding efficiency ratings and fuel prices. Natural gas furnaces are measured by AFUE (Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency), typically 80–98%, while electric heat pumps are measured by COP (Coefficient of Performance), typically 2.5–4.0, meaning they produce 250–400% more heat than the electricity they consume.

According to the U.S. Energy Information Administration, natural gas prices average $1.20 per therm nationally, while electricity averages $0.16 per kWh. However, these rates vary significantly by region — gas can be as low as $0.60/therm in gas-producing states like Texas or Louisiana, but over $2.00/therm in states without pipeline infrastructure.

When Electric Heat Pumps Win

  • Mild to Moderate Climates: In regions with heating seasons under 150 days, heat pumps often beat gas furnaces.
  • High Gas Prices: When gas exceeds $1.50/therm, heat pumps become more competitive.
  • Summer Cooling: Heat pumps provide free cooling, adding value beyond heating.
  • Clean Energy Regions: In areas with high renewable electricity, heat pumps have lower carbon footprint.

📊 Real Example: A 2,000 sq ft home in Ohio needs 60 million BTU/year for heating. A 95% AFUE gas furnace costs approximately $790/year to operate. A heat pump with COP 3.0 costs approximately $640/year — a 19% savings. For a full comparison, see our heat pump vs gas furnace cost comparison.

⚡ Pro Tip: Consider a dual-fuel system that switches between gas and electric based on outdoor temperature. This maximizes efficiency during shoulder seasons while ensuring reliability during extreme cold when heat pump efficiency drops.

Making the Right Fuel Choice

Choosing between gas and electric heating is one of the most consequential decisions a homeowner can make regarding long-term energy costs. Natural gas heating typically offers lower operating costs in regions where gas is available, with average annual heating bills 30-50% lower than electric resistance heating. However, electric heat pumps have advanced significantly in recent years, with cold-climate models maintaining efficient operation at outdoor temperatures as low as -13 degrees Fahrenheit. This calculator helps you compare the true cost of each option based on your local utility rates and heating requirements.

When comparing fuel options, consider more than just the cost per BTU. Natural gas furnaces have an average lifespan of 15-20 years and require annual maintenance, while heat pumps typically last 12-15 years but provide both heating and cooling from a single system. Installation costs also differ significantly: a gas furnace installation requires gas line connection and venting, while heat pump installation is generally simpler and may qualify for federal tax credits and utility rebates that reduce upfront costs by 15-30%. The U.S. Department of Energy offers comprehensive guidance on heat pump selection and installation.
