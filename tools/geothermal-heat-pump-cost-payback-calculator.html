About Geothermal Heat Pumps

A geothermal (ground-source) heat pump uses the earth's constant underground temperature (50-60°F) to heat and cool your home. While installation costs are high — typically $15,000 to $38,000 — the energy savings are substantial, ranging from 40% to 70% compared to traditional HVAC systems. The U.S. Department of Energy's geothermal heat pump guide covers system types, efficiency, and installation considerations.

Federal & State Incentives

In the US, the federal government offers a 30% tax credit with no dollar cap through the Inflation Reduction Act. Many states also offer additional rebates and incentives. This calculator automatically factors in federal tax credits and estimated state incentives based on your location.

💡 Pro Tip: Vertical loops cost 30-50% more than horizontal but require much less land. If you have less than 0.5 acres, vertical is likely your only option. However, vertical boreholes also access more stable ground temperatures, improving efficiency by 5-10%. For certified geothermal equipment, see the ENERGY STAR geothermal heat pump requirements.

⚠️ Common Mistake: Only comparing first-year savings. Geothermal systems last 20-25 years (loops last 50+). Even with a 7-12 year payback, you get 10-15 years of essentially free heating and cooling. Always calculate lifetime savings, not just payback.
