Why the Refrigerant Change?

The HVAC industry is undergoing its most significant refrigerant transition since the R-22 phase-out of the 2010s. Driven by the US AIM Act (American Innovation and Manufacturing Act of 2020) and the Kigali Amendment to the Montreal Protocol, the goal is to phase down hydrofluorocarbons (HFCs) with high global warming potential (GWP) in favor of lower-GWP alternatives. R-410A, the dominant refrigerant in residential HVAC for the past two decades, has a GWP of 2,088 — over 2,000 times more potent than carbon dioxide at trapping heat.

The timeline is straightforward: As of January 1, 2025, the US EPA prohibits manufacturing new HVAC equipment using refrigerants with GWP above 700. Canada followed with a similar ban on January 1, 2026. This means every new residential air conditioner and heat pump manufactured in or imported to North America after these dates uses one of the new A2L refrigerants — primarily R-32 (GWP 675) or R-454B (GWP 466).

For homeowners, this transition affects new purchases immediately, and it will gradually affect service costs for existing R-410A systems as the refrigerant supply declines. Understanding the changes helps you make informed decisions about when to repair versus replace, and what to expect from the new technology. What Are A2L Refrigerants & Why the Change?

A2L is the ASHRAE safety classification for refrigerants that are mildly flammable. The "A" indicates low toxicity (same as R-410A and R-22), the "2" indicates flammability (lower than A3 refrigerants like propane), and the "L" indicates low burning velocity — meaning the flame propagates slowly if ignited, at less than 4 inches per second.

The key environmental driver is Global Warming Potential (GWP). The new A2L refrigerants have GWP below 700, compared to R-410A at 2,088 and the old R-22 at 1,810. Over a 20-year period, replacing R-410A with R-32 reduces the climate impact of refrigerant leaks by approximately 68%. With R-454B (GWP 466), the reduction is approximately 78%.

For context, about 30% of HVAC system refrigerant leaks to the atmosphere over its lifetime through gradual permeation, service connections, and end-of-life recovery losses. The cumulative effect of switching 150 million US HVAC systems from R-410A to A2L refrigerants is equivalent to removing approximately 40 million cars from the road in terms of greenhouse gas reduction.

The Refrigerant Timeline

  • 1990s-2009: R-22 (HCFC) dominant. Phased out for new equipment in 2010, phased out for service in 2020.
  • 2010-2024: R-410A (HFC) dominant. GWP 2,088. Over 90% of residential systems use R-410A.
  • 2024: US EPA implements 40% production reduction of R-410A under AIM Act. Prices begin rising.
  • January 1, 2025: US ban on manufacturing new equipment with GWP > 700. All new US systems use A2L refrigerants.
  • January 1, 2026: Canada ban on manufacturing new equipment with GWP > 700. All new Canadian systems use A2L refrigerants.
  • 2029: 70% R-410A production reduction. Service refrigerant becomes significantly more expensive.
  • 2036: 85% R-410A production reduction. R-410A available only for critical service, at very high cost.

Both R-32 and R-454B meet the EPA GWP limit of 700 and are acceptable replacements for R-410A in new equipment. R-32 offers slightly better cooling capacity and efficiency (2-4% higher), while R-454B has lower GWP (466 vs 675) and may be less expensive to manufacture. For homeowners, the difference between the two is minimal — both will be common, both perform well, and both are supported by major manufacturers with long-term availability commitments.

It is important to understand that R-32 and R-454B are not interchangeable. A system designed for R-32 cannot use R-454B and vice versa. The refrigerant choice is determined by the equipment manufacturer, not the homeowner. When you choose a brand, you choose the refrigerant.

Safety Concerns & New Requirements

The mildly flammable (A2L) classification of the new refrigerants has generated the most questions and concerns from homeowners. Here is what you actually need to know:

How Flammable Are A2L Refrigerants?

A2L refrigerants require a specific concentration in air (13-14% by volume for R-32, 16-18% for R-454B) AND an ignition source to burn. For perspective, natural gas (methane) is flammable at 5-15% concentration and has a much higher burning velocity. The concentration of A2L refrigerant needed for ignition is approximately 4-5 times higher than what would occur from a typical HVAC system leak in a normally ventilated space.

Over 50 million R-32 air conditioning systems have been installed worldwide since 2012 (primarily in Japan, Europe, India, and Australia) with zero reported incidents of fire or explosion related to the A2L classification. This real-world safety record spans over a decade and multiple climate zones.

Mandatory Safety Features in New A2L Systems

New HVAC systems designed for A2L refrigerants include multiple layers of safety protection that were not present in R-410A systems:

  • Refrigerant leak detection sensors — installed in the indoor air handler or furnace cabinet, these sensors continuously monitor refrigerant concentration and trigger a shutdown if levels approach 25% of the lower flammability limit.
  • Automatic shutoff valves — if a leak is detected, valves isolate the outdoor unit refrigerant charge from the indoor section, limiting the amount of refrigerant that can leak indoors.
  • Enhanced electrical safety — all electrical components near refrigerant lines are sealed or rated for A2L environments. No exposed spark-producing components (relays, contactors) are permitted near potential leak points.
  • Pressure relief and ventilation — some installations require mechanical ventilation or specific room size minimums to ensure any leaked refrigerant dissipates below flammable concentrations.

Installation Requirements

Only technicians with EPA Section 608 certification (Type I, II, III) and additional A2L training can install, service, or handle A2L refrigerants. This training covers leak detection, safe handling, proper recovery equipment, and emergency procedures. The technician must follow manufacturer-specific installation instructions that include minimum room size calculations, ventilation requirements, and electrical safety checks.

Building codes have also been updated. The 2024 International Mechanical Code (IMC) includes specific provisions for A2L refrigerants, allowing their use in residential settings with the safety measures described above. Most US states and Canadian provinces have adopted these provisions as of 2025-2026.

Bottom line for homeowners: A2L systems are safe for residential installation, provided they are installed by trained, certified technicians following manufacturer specifications. The safety record is excellent, and the built-in safety features are robust.

Cost Impact on New HVAC Systems

The refrigerant transition has a direct impact on the cost of new HVAC equipment in 2026. Here is what you can expect:

Equipment Cost Increase

New A2L systems cost 5-15% more than equivalent R-410A systems due to:

  • Redesigned compressors: A2L-compatible compressors require different motor windings, terminal configurations, and internal safety controls to prevent spark ignition in the event of a refrigerant leak inside the compressor.
  • Leak detection sensors: Each indoor unit includes a refrigerant sensor ($50-$150 in component cost) that monitors air quality and triggers safety shutdowns.
  • Safety valves and controls: Automatic shutoff valves, enhanced electrical enclosures, and additional control boards add $100-$300 to the system cost.
  • Research and development amortization: Manufacturers have invested billions in developing new product lines, testing, and certification — costs that are passed to consumers in the first years of the transition.

R-410A Service Cost Increases

While existing R-410A systems can continue operating, the cost of service refrigerant is rising rapidly:

  • 2024: R-410A at $100-$150 per pound (retail, installed)
  • 2026: R-410A at $150-$250 per pound (due to 40% production reduction)
  • 2029: Projected $250-$400 per pound (70% production reduction)
  • 2034+: Projected $400-$600+ per pound (80%+ reduction)

For a typical 3-ton system holding 6-8 pounds of R-410A, a full recharge that cost $600-$900 in 2024 now costs $900-$1,800 in 2026. If your R-410A system develops a significant leak requiring a complete recharge, you should seriously consider replacement rather than repair. Retrofit Limitations

There is no approved retrofit path from R-410A to A2L refrigerants. You cannot "convert" an existing R-410A system to use R-32 or R-454B. The equipment is fundamentally different — compressors, expansion valves, heat exchangers, and safety controls are designed for specific refrigerants. Attempting a retrofit would void warranties, violate EPA regulations, and create safety and performance risks. If you want an A2L system, you must replace the entire matched system (outdoor unit, indoor coil, and air handler). What Homeowners Need to Know for 2026 Installs

If you are shopping for a new HVAC system in 2026, here is exactly what the refrigerant transition means for your purchase:

1. You Cannot Buy a New R-410A System

As of January 1, 2025 (US) and January 1, 2026 (Canada), manufacturers cannot produce new equipment using R-410A. Some existing inventory of R-410A systems may still be available from contractors who purchased stock before the ban, but these are limited. By mid-2026, virtually all new equipment sold uses A2L refrigerants. Do not seek out R-410A inventory — the technology is obsolete, and the refrigerant will become increasingly expensive to service.

2. A2L Systems Must Be Installed by Trained Technicians

Not all HVAC technicians are currently A2L-certified. When getting quotes, ask contractors if they have completed A2L handling training and have the required recovery equipment for A2L refrigerants. Reputable contractors serving the US and Canadian markets have been preparing for this transition since 2023 — if a contractor is not familiar with A2L requirements, find one who is.

3. Expect Minor Installation Location Considerations

In some installations — particularly in small rooms, basements without windows, or mechanical closets — the contractor may need to verify that the room volume meets minimum size requirements for A2L systems. The 2024 IMC allows A2L installations in most residential spaces, but there are specific restrictions for sleeping rooms without windows, continuous basement spaces, and rooms below grade with limited ventilation. Your contractor should perform this assessment as part of the quote process.

4. Consider the Service Implications

If you live in a remote area where HVAC service options are limited, verify that there are A2L-trained technicians available in your area before purchasing. It is unlikely to be a problem in most areas, but it is worth confirming. All major manufacturers have comprehensive A2L training programs, and most contractors completed this training in 2024-2025.

Contractor Questions Checklist for 2026

When interviewing HVAC contractors for a 2026 installation, ask these specific questions:

  • Are you certified to handle A2L (mildly flammable) refrigerants?
  • Do you have A2L-compatible recovery equipment?
  • Will the proposed system meet the room volume and ventilation requirements per manufacturer specifications?
  • Does the quote include the required leak detection sensor and safety shutoff valves?
  • What is the total system charge weight of A2L refrigerant, and how does it compare to the equivalent R-410A system?
  • Are there any additional building permit requirements for A2L installation in my jurisdiction?

Regional Rules: US States vs Canadian Provinces

While the federal refrigerant transition applies nationwide in both countries, individual states and provinces have adopted additional requirements or restrictions:

United States

California (most strict): The California Air Resources Board (CARB) has the most comprehensive refrigerant regulations beyond the federal requirements. In addition to the GWP < 700 requirement, California mandates: annual leak inspections for systems with 50+ pound charges (primarily commercial, but affects larger residential), enhanced refrigerant use reporting, and California-specific building code amendments (Title 24) that require additional ventilation in some A2L installations. California also restricts the use of R-454B in certain applications due to concerns about R-1234yf trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) breakdown products, though this remains under review.

New York: Follows the 2024 IMC with minor amendments requiring additional leak detection in multifamily buildings. No additional restrictions on A2L residential installations.

Massachusetts: Adopted 2024 IMC with specific requirements for A2L system registration and annual inspection reports. Residential installations are permitted with standard safety measures.

Washington, Oregon, Colorado: Have adopted stricter refrigerant management rules (reporting, leak repair timelines) but allow A2L residential installations per 2024 IMC standards.

Most other states: Follow the 2024 IMC or equivalent, allowing A2L installations with manufacturer-specified safety measures. No additional restrictions beyond the EPA federal requirements.

Canada

Federal (all provinces): Canada Ozone-Depleting Substances and Halocarbon Alternatives Regulations (ODS HAR) align with the Kigali Amendment. New equipment ban effective January 1, 2026. Requirements include: leak repair within 30 days for systems with 10+ kg charge, annual leak checks for systems with 50+ kg charge, and proper end-of-life recovery.

Quebec: Most stringent in Canada. Requires annual leak inspections for all systems with 3+ kg (6.6+ pounds) of refrigerant — this covers most residential heat pumps. Detailed record-keeping required. Quebec also has specific training requirements beyond federal certification.

British Columbia: Requires refrigerant handling certification for anyone purchasing or handling refrigerants. BC has adopted the National Building Code provisions for A2L systems with additional seismic bracing requirements.

Ontario: Follows federal regulations with building code provisions aligned to 2024 IMC standards. No additional provincial restrictions beyond federal requirements.

Alberta, Saskatchewan, Manitoba, Atlantic provinces: Follow federal regulations with standard building code adoption. No additional provincial restrictions.

Long-Term Benefits

Despite the short-term cost increases and transition challenges, the refrigerant change delivers significant long-term benefits:

  • Climate impact reduction: Full transition to A2L refrigerants in North America will reduce cumulative greenhouse gas emissions by an estimated 500 million metric tons of CO2 equivalent by 2040.
  • Technology advancement: The transition is driving compressor and system design improvements that would not have occurred with R-410A, including more efficient variable-speed compressors and advanced heat exchanger designs that improve overall system performance.
  • Regulatory stability: A2L refrigerants with GWP below 700 meet current and foreseeable regulatory requirements for at least the next 10-15 years, providing stability for equipment investments.
  • Global alignment: The US and Canada are now aligned with Europe, Japan, Australia, and most of Asia on refrigerant standards, enabling global technology sharing and lower long-term costs through scale.

Myth-Busting: Common Refrigerant Fears

Myth: "A2L refrigerants will explode like propane." Fact: A2L refrigerants have burning velocities below 4 inches/second — they burn, they do not explode. Propane (A3) has a burning velocity of 18 inches/second and a much wider flammability range. The two are not comparable.

Myth: "I will need to evacuate my home if there is a refrigerant leak." Fact: The leak detection sensors in A2L systems trigger a system shutdown at 25% of the lower flammability limit — well below the concentration needed for any ignition risk. In most cases, you will not even notice the leak; the system simply stops cooling and displays a diagnostic code for the technician.

Myth: "R-32 and R-454B are experimental refrigerants." Fact: R-32 has been used in air conditioning since 2012 and is installed in over 50 million systems worldwide. R-454B has been in use since 2020 and is approved by all major US HVAC manufacturers. Both are mature, well-tested refrigerants.

How to Choose Compatible New Systems

When buying a new HVAC system in 2026, here is how to navigate the refrigerant transition:

1. Choose Your Brand, Choose Your Refrigerant

Your refrigerant choice (R-32 vs R-454B) is determined by the brand you select:

  • R-32 systems: Daikin, Mitsubishi Electric, Fujitsu, LG, Goodman, Amana, Mr. Cool, Pioneer
  • R-454B systems: Carrier, Bryant, Lennox, Rheem, Ruud, Trane, American Standard, York, Coleman

Both refrigerants are well-supported and will have long-term parts and service availability. Do not make your brand decision based on the refrigerant — choose based on equipment features, warranty, local dealer support, and price.

2. Look for AHRI Certification

As with any HVAC purchase, insist on a matched system with a valid AHRI number. The AHRI directory now includes A2L refrigerant systems with specific SEER2, EER2, and HSPF2 ratings. Verify the system you are quoted is AHRI-certified with the listed efficiency ratings.

3. Verify Warranty Coverage

Most manufacturers offer standard 10-year parts warranties on A2L systems, matching their R-410A warranty terms. Some manufacturers include enhanced warranties (12-15 years) on their premium A2L product lines. Verify that the warranty explicitly covers the leak detection sensor and safety controls — these components are new and some manufacturers treat them as consumables.

4. Plan for the Future

Consider these factors when choosing an A2L system in 2026:

  • System lifespan: Your new system will serve you for 15-20 years. A2L refrigerants will be available and supported for the full life of the equipment.
  • Service availability: By 2026, over 80% of US and Canadian HVAC contractors have completed A2L training. Service availability is not a concern for most areas.
  • Resale value: Homes with modern A2L systems may have higher resale value compared to homes with aging R-410A systems that face expensive refrigerant costs.

Conclusion

The 2026 refrigerant transition from R-410A to A2L refrigerants (R-32 and R-454B) is a significant change for the HVAC industry, but for homeowners it is a manageable evolution rather than a disruption. The key points to remember:

  • Your existing R-410A system will continue working — there is no requirement to replace it.
  • New systems use A2L mildly flammable refrigerants that have an excellent global safety record (50M+ installations, zero fire incidents).
  • New A2L systems cost 5-15% more than equivalent R-410A systems, but offer 2-6% higher efficiency.
  • R-410A service costs are rising rapidly — if your old system needs a major refrigerant repair, replacement is increasingly the better option.
  • Choose your brand, and the refrigerant (R-32 or R-454B) comes with it. Both are excellent, well-supported options.
  • Work with A2L-trained contractors who can verify installation compliance with safety requirements.
  • Federal tax credits and utility rebates can help offset the cost of new high-efficiency A2L systems.
