Why SEER2 Replaced SEER

In January 2023, the US Department of Energy (DOE) introduced SEER2 — a new efficiency rating standard that replaced the decades-old SEER system. This was the most significant change to HVAC efficiency ratings in over 30 years, and it directly affects every homeowner buying a new air conditioner or heat pump in 2026.

The shift to SEER2 was driven by a simple problem: the old SEER test did not reflect real-world conditions. The old test used the A2 standard — testing the air handler without a filter, with unrestricted ductwork, and at ideal static pressure. In reality, every HVAC system works against filters, duct restrictions, and less-than-perfect installation conditions. SEER2 uses the M1 standard, which includes a filter and more realistic static pressure, producing ratings that better match what homeowners actually experience.

The practical impact is straightforward: SEER2 ratings are roughly 4-5% lower than old SEER ratings for the same equipment. A system that would have been rated SEER 16 under the old test now rates approximately SEER2 15.2-15.4. This does not mean the equipment is less efficient — the test is simply more realistic. It means you cannot directly compare a SEER2 number to an old SEER number when evaluating systems.

For homeowners shopping in 2026, understanding SEER2 is essential. You will only see SEER2 ratings on new equipment. Old SEER-rated systems can no longer be manufactured or imported into the US (though existing stock can still be sold). Every new central air conditioner and heat pump sold in the US today carries a SEER2 label. Our heat pump installation cost guide covers pricing for all system types covered by the new standard.

State-Level Requirements

Some states have adopted higher minimum SEER2 requirements than the federal minimums:

  • California: Requires SEER2 15.0 minimum (matches Southwest federal), but California Energy Commission (CEC) standards effectively require higher performance for new construction — typically SEER2 17+ for most installations.
  • New York: SEER2 14.0 minimum for new installations (above the 13.4 federal Northern minimum), with a goal of SEER2 16+ by 2028.
  • Massachusetts: SEER2 14.5 minimum through state building code amendments, aligning with Mass Save incentive requirements.
  • Washington State: SEER2 14.0 minimum effective 2025, moving to SEER2 15.0 in 2027.
  • Oregon: SEER2 14.0 minimum for new construction, with heat pump requirements for new homes starting 2026.
  • Colorado: SEER2 14.0 minimum effective 2025 for new construction, with higher requirements for heat pump installations.

These state-level requirements may change — always check with your local HVAC contractor for the most current requirements in your area before purchasing equipment.

What Happens if You Install Below-Minimum Equipment?

Installing equipment that does not meet the federal minimum SEER2 for your region is illegal for licensed contractors and voids the AHRI certification. You will not be able to obtain permits for new construction or replacements. In practice, HVAC distributors will not sell below-minimum equipment to contractors, so homeowners cannot accidentally purchase non-compliant systems.

SEER2 for Heat Pumps & Air Conditioners

SEER2 applies to both air conditioners and heat pumps in cooling mode. However, heat pump buyers need to consider additional factors beyond just the SEER2 number:

Heat Pumps: SEER2 + HSPF2 Together

A heat pump has two efficiency ratings: SEER2 (cooling) and HSPF2 (heating). These are independent — a heat pump can have excellent SEER2 but mediocre HSPF2, or vice versa. When comparing heat pumps, always look at both ratings together. In cold climates (Canada, Northeast, Upper Midwest), HSPF2 is more important than SEER2 because your system spends 2-5 times more hours heating than cooling.

Example comparison: Heat Pump A has SEER2 20 / HSPF2 7.5. Heat Pump B has SEER2 18 / HSPF2 9.0. In Minnesota, Heat Pump B will save more money overall because its superior heating efficiency offsets the slightly lower cooling efficiency during the short summer. In Florida, Heat Pump A would be the better choice. Our heat pump vs gas furnace comparison provides a detailed cost breakdown across different climates and fuel prices.

Communicating vs Non-Communicating Systems

Modern high-SEER2 heat pumps (SEER2 18+) use communicating technology — the indoor unit, outdoor unit, and thermostat continuously exchange data to optimize operation. These systems adjust compressor speed, fan speed, and expansion valve position in real time to maintain the exact temperature with minimum energy. A communicating SEER2 18 system typically outperforms a non-communicating SEER2 20 system in real-world use because its intelligence compensates for the lower rated efficiency.

For heat pumps specifically, communicating controls dramatically improve cold-weather performance. The system can actively manage defrost cycles, reduce compressor speed to match low load conditions, and optimize the balance between heat pump and auxiliary heat. This is why cold-climate heat pumps like the Carrier Greenspeed or Lennox SL28XCV achieve such impressive real-world efficiencies despite moderate SEER2 ratings.

Variable-Speed vs Single-Stage

High SEER2 systems almost always use variable-speed (inverter) compressors and fans. These systems adjust output from 25% to 100% capacity rather than running at full power and cycling on and off. The benefits go beyond efficiency — variable-speed systems provide better humidity control, quieter operation, and more consistent temperatures. They also achieve their rated SEER2 more consistently because they can adapt to different load conditions.

Single-stage and two-stage compressors are typically found on SEER2 13.4-16 systems. While adequate for basic cooling, they cannot match the comfort and efficiency of variable-speed systems. If your budget allows, choose a variable-speed system even if it means accepting a marginally lower SEER2 number — the real-world performance will be better.

Myth-Busting: "Higher SEER2 Is Always Better"

Higher SEER2 is not always the best financial decision. Our energy efficient HVAC systems guide explores when premium efficiency makes financial sense. Here is when it makes sense — and when it does not:

When higher SEER2 is worth it: You live in a hot climate (1,500+ cooling hours/year), your electricity rates are above $0.14/kWh, you plan to stay in your home 7+ years, your existing ductwork is in good condition, and you can access federal tax credits and utility rebates to offset the premium.

When it is not worth it: You live in a cool climate (under 800 cooling hours), you plan to move within 5 years, your ductwork needs significant repairs (which should be prioritized), or your electricity rates are very low (under $0.10/kWh). In these cases, a SEER2 15-16 system delivers the best value.

Hidden cost of high SEER2: High-efficiency systems have more complex components — variable-speed drives, electronic expansion valves, advanced control boards — that can be expensive to repair if they fail outside warranty. A standard SEER2 14 system may cost less to repair over its lifetime than a premium SEER2 26 system, even though it costs more to operate.

EER2, HSPF2 & Other Related Ratings

SEER2 is the most widely discussed HVAC efficiency rating, but it is not the only one that matters. Here is how the other ratings work and when they are important:

EER2 — Energy Efficiency Ratio 2

EER2 measures cooling efficiency at a single peak-load condition (95°F outdoor, 80°F indoor, highest compressor speed). While SEER2 measures average efficiency across a cooling season, EER2 measures maximum efficiency under the hottest conditions. EER2 is important for two reasons:

  • Hot climates: In Phoenix, Las Vegas, Texas, and Arizona, your AC operates at peak conditions for extended periods. A system with high SEER2 but low EER2 will perform worse than the SEER2 number suggests during heat waves.
  • Utility rebate requirements: Many utility rebates require both minimum SEER2 and minimum EER2. For example, a typical rebate might require SEER2 16+ and EER2 12+.

As a rule of thumb, look for EER2 that is at least 65-70% of the SEER2 rating. A SEER2 18 system with EER2 12 is well-balanced. A SEER2 18 system with EER2 9.5 should be avoided in hot climates.

HSPF2 — Heating Seasonal Performance Factor 2

HSPF2 is the heating efficiency rating for heat pumps, using the same M1 testing standard as SEER2. It measures how efficiently a heat pump produces heat across a typical heating season (outdoor temperatures from 17°F to 62°F).

Minimum HSPF2 requirements in 2026 are 6.7 for split system heat pumps and 6.0 for packaged units. ENERGY STAR certified heat pumps must achieve HSPF2 7.5 or higher. For cold climates, look for HSPF2 8.5+ — these systems maintain high efficiency even in below-freezing conditions.

The relationship between HSPF2 and SEER2 varies by product line. Some manufacturers optimize for cooling (high SEER2, moderate HSPF2), while others prioritize heating performance (high HSPF2, moderate SEER2). Always check both ratings when buying a heat pump.

COP — Coefficient of Performance

COP measures instantaneous heating or cooling efficiency at a specific temperature. A COP of 3.0 means the heat pump produces 3 units of heat for every 1 unit of electricity consumed. While COP is not used as a standard rating for residential equipment, it is often cited for cold-climate heat pump performance at low temperatures. A good cold-climate heat pump should maintain COP above 2.0 at 5°F and above 1.5 at -13°F.

How to Verify & Buy the Right System in 2026

With multiple ratings, regional requirements, and manufacturer claims, buying the right HVAC system in 2026 requires careful verification. Here is your step-by-step buying guide:

Step 1: Check the AHRI Directory

Every matched HVAC system sold in the US must be certified by AHRI (Air-Conditioning, Heating, and Refrigeration Institute). Before purchasing, ask your contractor for the AHRI number of the proposed system (outdoor unit + indoor coil + air handler combination). Enter this number at ahridirectory.org to verify the official SEER2, EER2, and HSPF2 ratings. If the contractor cannot provide an AHRI number, the system is not properly matched and will not achieve the advertised efficiency.

Warning: Some contractors propose mixing brands or using non-matched components. A Carrier outdoor unit with a Goodman indoor coil is NOT AHRI certified and will underperform. Always insist on matched, AHRI-certified combinations.

Step 2: Understand the Yellow EnergyGuide Label

Every new HVAC system sold in the US includes a yellow EnergyGuide label. This label shows the SEER2 rating, estimated annual energy cost, and a comparison scale showing how the system ranks against similar products. The estimated cost is based on national average electricity rates and typical usage — your actual cost will vary. Use the SEER2 number on the label, not the cost estimate, to compare systems.

Step 3: Get Multiple Quotes with AHRI Numbers

Obtain at least three quotes from licensed, insured HVAC contractors. Each quote must include the specific model numbers for all components (outdoor unit, indoor coil, air handler/furnace) and the AHRI number for the matched system. Do not accept partial model numbers or generic descriptions like "16 SEER2 system."

Step 4: Factor in Rebates and Tax Credits

The 2026 federal tax credit (Inflation Reduction Act) provides up to $2,000 for systems meeting specific efficiency thresholds:

  • $2,000 tax credit for heat pumps meeting SEER2 15.2+ and HSPF2 8.5+ (split systems)
  • $600 tax credit for air conditioners meeting SEER2 16+
  • $150 tax credit for ENERGY STAR smart thermostats

State and utility rebates add $200-$1,500 depending on your location. Use the DSIRE database to find incentives in your area. Our HVAC tax credits and rebates guide explains how to qualify and claim each incentive.

Step 5: Verify Installation Quality

A high-SEER2 system installed poorly will perform worse than a basic system installed properly. Ensure your contractor performs these critical steps:

  • Manual J load calculation — proper sizing based on your home heat gain/loss
  • Manual D duct design — confirm your ductwork can handle the required airflow
  • Refrigerant charge verification — weighed-in charge for optimal performance
  • Airflow measurement — using a flow hood or anemometer to verify 350-450 CFM per ton
  • Static pressure test — confirm total external static pressure is within equipment limits

Conclusion

SEER2 efficiency ratings in 2026 are more realistic and accurate than old SEER ratings, giving homeowners better information for making purchasing decisions. The key takeaways:

  • SEER2 ratings are 4-5% lower than old SEER ratings — SEER2 15 equals approximately SEER 16 under the old standard
  • Minimum requirements vary by region: SEER2 13.4 in the North, SEER2 15 in the South
  • Each SEER2 point saves 6-8% on cooling energy consumption
  • SEER2 15-17 offers the best value for most homeowners
  • High-SEER2 systems (20+) make financial sense in hot climates with high electricity rates
  • For heat pumps, consider HSPF2 alongside SEER2 — heating efficiency matters more in cold climates
  • Always verify AHRI certification and insist on professional installation
  • Federal tax credits and utility rebates can offset 20-40% of the system cost
