Gas Furnace vs Electric Furnace: 2026 Costs & Efficiency

Gas furnace vs electric furnace — 2026 operating costs, efficiency ratings, installation prices, and which is cheaper to run in your region.

The choice between a gas furnace and an electric furnace comes down to one question more than any other: what is cheaper to run where you live? The answer in 2026 is clear for most of North America — natural gas remains significantly cheaper than electricity for heating. But that is not the whole story. Installation costs, equipment availability, home value, and environmental considerations all factor into the decision.

Energy prices shifted notably in 2026. Natural gas prices stabilized after the volatility of 2022-2024, settling at roughly $13-$18 per million BTUs across most US markets. Electricity rates continued their steady climb, now averaging 14-17 cents per kilowatt-hour nationally, with some markets exceeding 30 cents. These trends matter because the operating cost gap between gas and electric heat has widened in favor of gas over the past three years.

This guide compares gas and electric furnaces across every dimension that matters: upfront cost, ongoing operating expenses, efficiency, maintenance, lifespan, and environmental impact. By the end, you will know exactly which type fits your home and budget.

Upfront Installation Cost Comparison

The first number you see when comparing furnaces is the installation price, and electric furnaces win this round decisively. Electric furnaces are simpler machines with fewer components, and they do not require gas piping, flue venting, or combustion air provisions. Efficiency Comparison

Efficiency is measured differently for gas and electric furnaces, which causes confusion. Let us clear that up.

Gas furnace efficiency is measured by AFUE (Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency). A 96% AFUE gas furnace converts 96% of the fuel into heat, with only 4% lost up the flue. The best gas furnaces in 2026 reach 98-98.5% AFUE. No gas furnace can reach 100% because some heat always escapes with combustion exhaust.

Electric furnace efficiency is effectively 100% because all the electricity that enters the unit converts directly to heat. There is no exhaust to lose heat, no combustion process to waste fuel. Every watt becomes heat inside your home.

This makes electric furnaces more efficient at converting their fuel source into heat. However, fuel efficiency is only half the equation. The other half is cost efficiency. Natural gas delivers roughly 3 times more heat per dollar than electricity in most markets. A 96% AFUE gas furnace turns $1 of fuel into about $0.96 worth of heat. An electric furnace turns $1 of electricity into $1 worth of heat, but that $1 of electricity buys only about one-third as much heat as $1 of natural gas. Maintenance and Lifespan

Gas Furnace Maintenance

Gas furnaces require annual professional maintenance to operate safely and efficiently. A typical service visit costs $100-$200 and includes cleaning the burners, inspecting the heat exchanger for cracks, checking gas pressure, testing safety controls, and replacing the air filter. Annual maintenance is not optional for gas furnaces — it is essential for safety. A cracked heat exchanger can leak carbon monoxide into your home, creating a serious health hazard.

Gas furnace lifespan averages 15-20 years with proper maintenance. The heat exchanger is the critical component that determines the furnace's useful life. Most manufacturers offer lifetime heat exchanger warranties, but labor costs for replacement can exceed $2,000 if the heat exchanger fails after the warranty expires.

Electric Furnace Maintenance

Electric furnaces require significantly less maintenance than gas furnaces. There are no burners to clean, no heat exchanger to inspect, no flue to check. Maintenance consists primarily of replacing the air filter regularly and checking electrical connections. An annual inspection is still recommended but is less critical than for gas furnaces.

Electric furnace lifespan averages 20-30 years, significantly longer than gas furnaces. The simpler design means fewer components that can fail. When electric furnaces do fail, the problem is usually a heating element, limit switch, or relay — all relatively inexpensive repairs compared to gas furnace components.

Performance Comparison

How do these furnaces perform in real-world conditions? Here is what matters day to day:

Heat output: Gas furnaces produce hotter supply air (typically 130-140 degrees Fahrenheit) compared to electric furnaces (100-120 degrees). This means gas furnaces heat a home faster and recover temperature more quickly after the thermostat drops. In very cold weather, gas furnaces maintain comfort more effectively.

Temperature consistency: Modern two-stage and modulating gas furnaces provide more consistent temperatures than electric furnaces because they can vary their heat output. Standard electric furnaces operate in a simple on-off cycle, creating the same temperature swings as a single-stage gas furnace.

Power outages: Gas furnaces require electricity to run the blower motor and controls, so they do not work during power outages unless you have a backup generator. Electric furnaces also stop working without power. However, some gas furnaces can be paired with low-wattage generators more easily than electric furnaces.

Ductwork requirements: Both furnace types use the same ductwork. Switching from electric to gas (or vice versa) rarely requires duct modifications unless the new furnace has different airflow requirements.

Environmental Impact

The environmental comparison between gas and electric furnaces depends heavily on where your electricity comes from.

Natural gas furnaces burn fossil fuel directly in your home, producing carbon dioxide and nitrogen oxides. A 96% AFUE gas furnace produces about 13 pounds of CO2 per therm of natural gas burned. For a typical northern home, that translates to roughly 4-6 tons of CO2 emissions per year.

Electric furnaces produce zero emissions at the point of use, but the power plant that generates the electricity may produce significant emissions. In regions where electricity comes from coal or natural gas, an electric furnace can produce more total emissions than a high-efficiency gas furnace. In regions with hydro, nuclear, wind, or solar generation, electric furnaces produce far fewer emissions.

The cleanest option for most homeowners is a heat pump, which is 2-4 times more efficient than electric resistance heating and produces the lowest emissions when paired with renewable electricity. If you are choosing between gas and electric resistance, gas is generally better for both your wallet and the environment unless you live in a region with very clean grid electricity.

When to Choose Each Type

Choose a gas furnace when:

  • Natural gas is available at your property
  • You live in a cold climate with more than 4,000 heating degree days
  • You want the lowest possible heating bills
  • You plan to stay in your home for 5+ years
  • You already have gas appliances (water heater, stove)

Choose an electric furnace when:

  • Natural gas is not available in your area
  • You live in a very mild climate with minimal heating needs
  • Your electricity rates are below 8 cents per kWh
  • You need a backup heat source for a heat pump system
  • You want the lowest upfront investment

Choose a heat pump instead of either when:

  • You live in a moderate climate (zones 1-4)
  • You need both heating and cooling
  • You want to maximize energy efficiency
  • You want to claim the $2,000 federal tax credit
  • You want to reduce your carbon footprint

Conclusion: Which Furnace Should You Buy in 2026?

For the vast majority of North American homeowners, the gas furnace is the better choice. The upfront cost is higher, but the operating cost savings are substantial and accumulate every month of every winter. A 96% AFUE gas furnace installed in a cold-climate home saves $1,500-$2,400 per year compared to an electric furnace. Over 15 years, that translates to $22,000-$36,000 in total savings. The installation cost difference pays back in 2-4 years, and everything after that is money in your pocket. Compare top furnace brands for 2026 to find the best option for your home.

Electric furnaces make sense only in specific situations: no gas available, very mild climate, or extremely cheap electricity. If you fall into one of those categories, an electric furnace works fine. But for most homeowners in most regions, the long-term math favors gas strongly.

If you have access to natural gas and live in a cold climate, do not let the higher installation cost of a gas furnace scare you off. The monthly savings on your heating bill will more than compensate. If you are building a new home or replacing both heating and cooling systems, look seriously at heat pumps first. They offer the best combination of efficiency, environmental performance, and long-term value for many homeowners in 2026.
