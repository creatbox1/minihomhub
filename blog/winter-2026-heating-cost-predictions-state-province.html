Energy Price Outlook for Winter 2026

Winter 2026-2027 is shaping up to be a period of moderate heating cost increases across most of North America, driven by stable natural gas prices, modest electricity rate hikes, and an expected colder-than-average winter in the eastern US and Canada due to a developing La Niña pattern. The National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA) winter outlook projects a 55-65% probability of below-average temperatures in the Northeast, Midwest, and Pacific Northwest, with above-average temperatures likely in the South and Southwest. Heating costs also vary significantly by home type — our townhome and apartment HVAC cost guide breaks down these differences.

For the average US household, winter heating costs are projected to range from $450-$1,400 depending on fuel type, climate zone, home size, and efficiency. Canadian households face $600-$2,200 CAD for the November-March heating season. Use our heating cost calculator to estimate your specific winter bills based on your home size, fuel type, and location. These projections are based on the Energy Information Administration (EIA) Short-Term Energy Outlook, Natural Resources Canada energy price forecasts, and analysis of regional climate and housing data.

This guide provides state-by-state and province-by-province heating cost predictions for winter 2026-2027, explains the key factors driving costs in your region, and delivers actionable strategies to reduce your heating bills regardless of where you live. Key Influencing Factors

1. La Niña Winter Pattern

The Climate Prediction Center indicates a 60% probability of a La Niña pattern developing by November 2026, typically bringing colder-than-average temperatures to the northern US and Canada and warmer-than-average conditions to the southern US. This means the Northeast, Midwest, and Pacific Northwest are likely to see 5-10% more heating degree days (HDD) than average, while the South and Southwest may see 5-10% fewer HDD. Every 1% increase in HDD translates to approximately 1% higher heating costs.

2. Natural Gas Prices

Natural gas is the most common heating fuel in both the US (47% of homes) and Canada (44%). The EIA projects Henry Hub natural gas prices of $2.80-$3.50 per MMBtu for winter 2026-2027 — a moderate increase from the $2.50-$3.00 range of winter 2025-2026. This increase is driven by LNG export demand and modest production growth. Because natural gas is a globally traded commodity, geopolitical events (conflict in Europe or the Middle East) could push prices significantly higher — the "high scenario" assumes $4.00+/MMBtu.

3. Electricity Rates

US average electricity rates are projected at $0.14-$0.15/kWh for winter 2026-2027, up 3% year over year. Rates vary dramatically by state: Washington $0.10, Texas $0.12, Colorado $0.13, Ohio $0.14, New York $0.21, California $0.28, Hawaii $0.40. Homes with heat pumps benefit from this stability — at COP 3.0, heat pump heating costs $0.05/kWh equivalent, cheaper than any fossil fuel. Our SEER2 efficiency ratings guide explains how modern heat pumps achieve these performance levels. Homes with electric resistance heat face the highest costs at $0.14-$0.28/kWh.

4. Heating Oil & Propane

Approximately 5% of US homes (mostly in the Northeast and Midwest) and 15% of Canadian homes (concentrated in Atlantic Canada and rural areas) rely on heating oil. Winter 2026-2027 oil prices are projected at $3.40-$3.80/gallon — a 5-8% increase driven by global crude oil markets and refinery capacity. Propane users (5% of US homes, primarily in the Midwest and Southeast) face $2.70-$3.10/gallon. Both fuels are projected to cost 2-4x more per BTU than natural gas or heat pumps.

5. Home Efficiency

The single most variable factor in winter heating costs is home efficiency. Two identical homes in the same climate can have heating bills differing by 40-60% based solely on insulation levels, air sealing, window quality, and HVAC efficiency. The medium scenario assumes average insulation (R-19 to R-30 attic, single-pane or old double-pane windows) and standard-efficiency equipment (80% AFUE furnace or SEER2 14 heat pump). Homes with R-49+ attic insulation and 95%+ efficient equipment will see costs 25-40% below the medium projection, as recommended by the Department of Energy.

Frequently Asked Questions

The average US household will spend $520-$900 for winter heating (November-March), depending on climate and fuel prices. Canadian households face $800-$1,350 CAD. Heating oil users in the Northeast may pay $1,200-$2,200, while natural gas users average $550-$850.

Heat pumps are the most cost-effective heating option in 2026, with an average seasonal COP of 3.0 providing heating at roughly $0.05/kWh equivalent — cheaper than natural gas ($0.08-$0.12/therm equivalent), heating oil ($0.12-$0.16 equivalent), or propane ($0.14-$0.18 equivalent). Natural gas is the cheapest fossil fuel option.

Seal air leaks around windows, doors, and attic hatches; add attic insulation to R-49 or higher; install a programmable thermostat; service your heating system annually; and use ceiling fans in reverse (clockwise) to circulate warm air. These measures can reduce heating costs by 25-40%.

Natural gas prices are projected to increase modestly by 4% compared to winter 2025-2026, with Henry Hub prices of $2.80-$3.50 per MMBtu. This increase is driven by LNG export demand and modest production growth. Geopolitical events could push prices significantly higher in a high-scenario case.

Alaska has the highest projected winter heating costs at $1,200-$2,100, followed by New England states at $800-$1,350. In Canada, Atlantic provinces (Nova Scotia, Newfoundland & Labrador) have the highest costs at $1,100-$2,000 CAD due to heating oil dependency and cold climates.
