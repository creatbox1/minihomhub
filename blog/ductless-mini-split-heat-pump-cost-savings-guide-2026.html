I tell homeowners this all the time: if your house does not have ductwork, do not build ducts. Install a ductless mini split instead. In 2026, that advice is more relevant than ever. Mini splits have outsold central heat pumps in the US for three consecutive years. The combination of zone control, no duct losses, falling equipment prices, and generous heat pump rebates and incentives has made them the default choice for millions of homeowners.

I have audited homes with mini splits from Maine to British Columbia. I have seen them heat 200-year-old farmhouses in Vermont and cool glass-walled condos in Vancouver. This guide covers everything — cost per zone, comparisons with central systems, payback periods, top brands, and where mini splits make the most sense. Installation Cost by Indoor Unit Type

The type of indoor unit also affects price:

  • Wall-mounted: $800-$1,500 per unit — most common, most efficient airflow, lowest cost.
  • Ceiling cassette: $1,200-$2,000 per unit — flush mount, best for open spaces, more expensive installation.
  • Floor-mounted: $1,000-$1,800 per unit — good for low windows or homes where wall mounting is not possible.
  • Ducted (ceiling-hideaway): $1,500-$2,500 per unit — hides in ceiling/attic, serves multiple small rooms via short ducts, most expensive.

Cost Factors & Detailed Breakdown

Here is where the money goes for a typical 3-zone mini split installation at $10,000 total:

  • Equipment (45-55%): $4,500 to $5,500. Includes outdoor condenser, 3 indoor air handlers, line sets, wireless controllers, and mounting hardware.
  • Labor (25-35%): $2,500 to $3,500. Includes mounting indoor units, running line sets through walls/crawlspace, electrical connections, vacuum evacuation, refrigerant charging, and system commissioning.
  • Electrical work (8-15%): $800 to $1,500. Includes dedicated circuit from panel, disconnect switch, and wiring to outdoor unit.
  • Permits and inspections (2-5%): $200 to $500. Varies by municipality.
  • Miscellaneous (3-8%): $300 to $800. Condensate pumps, line hide covers, extra refrigerant line, crane/boom truck for difficult outdoor unit placement.

The three biggest variables that shift this breakdown:

Line set length. Each zone needs a refrigerant line from outdoor to indoor unit. Standard runs under 50 feet are included. Runs over 75 feet cost $10-$20 per additional foot. Long runs also reduce efficiency, so keep them short.

Wall construction. Drilling through brick, stone, or concrete costs more than wood frame. Add $200-$500 per penetration for masonry.

Outdoor unit placement. Ground-level installation on a concrete pad is cheapest. Roof-mount or balcony installations requiring a crane add $500 to $2,000.

Best Applications for Ductless Mini Splits

Mini splits are not the right solution for every home. Based on my field experience, here is where they excel and where you should look elsewhere.

Excellent Matches

  • Homes without existing ductwork: This is the #1 reason to choose mini splits. Building ducts in an existing home costs $4,000-$8,000 and requires significant demolition. Mini splits avoid this entirely.
  • Older homes (pre-1950): Many older homes have thick plaster walls, no insulation, and no ductwork. Mini splits provide efficient HVAC without structural modification. I have installed them in Victorian, Craftsman, and mid-century modern homes with outstanding results.
  • Room additions, sunrooms, and garages: Extending existing ductwork to a new addition rarely makes sense. A single-zone mini split serves an addition for $3,500-$6,000 with independent temperature control.
  • Multi-generational homes: Different generations often prefer different temperatures. Mini splits let each zone operate independently — one room at 72°F, another at 65°F, with no fighting over the thermostat.
  • Homes with hydronic (radiator) heat and no AC: If you have hot water radiators for heat and want to add cooling without installing ductwork, mini splits are the perfect solution. They provide efficient cooling and can supplement or replace the boiler for heating.

Consider a Ducted System Instead

  • Homes with existing ductwork in good condition: If your ducts are already there and leak-free, a ducted heat pump is simpler and often cheaper than installing mini splits in every room.
  • Very large open floor plans: A single open great room may need 2-3 mini split heads for proper coverage. One larger ducted unit often does the same job for less.
  • Homeowner association restrictions: Some HOAs restrict the visible wall-mounted units that mini splits require. Ducted systems with vents only may be the only option.
  • Rentals or short-term ownership: The upfront cost of a multi-zone mini split may not recover before you sell. A window AC or portable unit may be more practical for a 3-year horizon.

Maintenance, Lifespan & Hidden Costs

Routine Maintenance

Mini splits require more user involvement than central systems because each indoor unit has its own filter. Clean or replace filters every 1-3 months — it takes 5 minutes and requires no tools. Annual professional service ($150-$250) should include:

  • Deep cleaning of indoor coils and condensate pans (mold prevention)
  • Refrigerant pressure and temperature checks
  • Electrical connection inspection
  • Condensate drain line flush
  • Outdoor unit coil cleaning

Lifespan

12 to 18 years with proper maintenance. The outdoor unit typically fails first — usually the compressor or fan motor. Indoor units often outlast the outdoor unit. When the outdoor unit fails after 12-15 years, you can replace just the outdoor unit and keep the indoor units if they are still in good condition, provided the new outdoor unit is compatible.

Hidden Costs to Budget For

  • Electrical panel upgrade: $500-$3,000. Single-zone mini splits often run on existing 120V circuits. Multi-zone systems may need a dedicated 240V circuit and possibly a panel upgrade.
  • Condensate pumps: $150-$300 each. Needed when the indoor unit is below grade or cannot drain by gravity.
  • Line hide covers: $100-$400. Plastic channels that conceal refrigerant lines on exterior walls. Not strictly necessary but greatly improve aesthetics.
  • Wall sleeves for masonry: $200-$500 per penetration when drilling through brick, stone, or concrete.

Ductless Mini Split Buyer Checklist

Before you sign a contract, confirm each of these items:

  1. Manual J load calculation completed. Not a square-footage rule of thumb. Insist on a proper heat load calculation for each room.
  2. Line set runs under 75 feet per zone. Longer runs reduce efficiency and increase cost. The contractor should minimize run distances.
  3. Condensate drainage plan for each indoor unit. Gravity drain is best. If not possible, confirm a condensate pump is included.
  4. Model numbers confirmed on ENERGY STAR qualified list. Verify the specific model qualifies for rebates before installation.
  5. Contractor is licensed, insured, and registered with rebate programs. Check that they participate in your target state/provincial rebate programs.
  6. Electrical work included in quote. Get a line item for the dedicated circuit and any panel work. Do not assume it is included.
  7. Warranty details in writing. Compressor, parts, and labor warranties. Premium brands offer 10-12 years on compressors.
  8. Rebate paperwork responsibility assigned. Will the contractor submit applications, or is it your responsibility?
  9. Three references from similar installations. Call them. Ask about winter performance, noise, and contractor responsiveness.
  10. Post-installation commissioning. The contractor should run the system through heating and cooling mode, check all temperatures, and review operation with you.

When to Choose a Ductless Mini Split in 2026

Here is my bottom-line recommendation after 340+ audits and hundreds of mini split installations reviewed:

Choose a ductless mini split if: Your home has no existing ductwork, you want independent zone control, you are heating or cooling an addition or single room, or you have hydronic heat and want to add efficient cooling. Mini splits deliver the best combination of efficiency, comfort, and cost for these scenarios.

Choose a ducted central heat pump if: Your home already has ductwork in good condition, you prefer invisible HVAC equipment, or you have a large open floor plan where a single system can cover the space efficiently.

Consider a combination approach if: You have existing ductwork but need to add conditioned space (an addition, garage, or bonus room). Install a central heat pump for the main house and a single-zone mini split for the addition. This hybrid approach often delivers the best of both worlds.
