You just spent $7,000 on a new furnace. The contractor tells you it comes with a 10-year warranty. You feel relieved. But what does that warranty actually cover when something breaks? The answer is less than most homeowners think.

HVAC warranties are one of the most misunderstood aspects of buying new equipment. Homeowners assume their investment is fully protected, only to discover on a cold winter night that the "warranty" only covers the compressor while the $600 labor bill is entirely their responsibility. Understanding exactly what each brand covers, what registration requirements apply, and what can void your coverage saves you from expensive surprises.

This guide breaks down every major HVAC brand's warranty terms for 2026, explains the difference between parts and labor coverage, and tells you exactly what you need to do to keep your warranty valid.

Disclaimer This guide is for educational purposes. Warranty terms change periodically. Verify current terms with the manufacturer or your contractor before purchase.

How HVAC Warranties Actually Work

Every HVAC warranty has three distinct layers. Understanding each one prevents surprises when you need service.

Manufacturer Parts Warranty: This covers the cost of replacing defective components in your HVAC system. If the compressor fails, the manufacturer sends a free replacement compressor to your contractor. You pay nothing for the part. This warranty covers the compressor, heat exchanger, blower motor, control board, and other internal components that fail due to manufacturing defects.

Manufacturer Labor Warranty: This does not exist as a standard offering from any major HVAC brand. Manufacturers cover parts only. When a warranty repair is needed, you pay the contractor's labor rate to install the replacement part. Labor rates range from $100 to $250 per hour, and a typical warranty repair takes 2-4 hours. That $0 part can cost you $300-$800 to have installed.

Contractor Labor Warranty: The installing contractor typically warranties their workmanship for 1-2 years. This covers installation errors like incorrect gas pressure, improper refrigerant charge, loose electrical connections, or ductwork issues. If the system stops working because of how it was installed, the contractor fixes it at no charge during this period.

Extended labor warranties (also called labor service plans) can be purchased from the contractor or manufacturer to extend labor coverage beyond the first year. These cost $300-$800 for 5-10 years of additional labor coverage.

Registered vs. Non-Registered Warranty

HVAC warranties are designed to be registered. If you fail to register your product within the specified window, your coverage is dramatically reduced. This is the single most important thing to understand about HVAC warranties, and the one most homeowners get wrong.

Failing to register reduces your parts and compressor coverage from 10 years to just 5 years across every major brand. Some brands also reduce heat exchanger coverage from lifetime to 20 years if not registered. This single failure can cost you thousands of dollars in uncovered repairs during years 6-10 of your system's life.

How to register: After installation, find the model and serial numbers on the data plate of your outdoor condenser or furnace cabinet. Go to the manufacturer's website and locate the warranty registration page. Enter your information and the product details. You will receive a confirmation email. Keep this with your homeowner records. Most contractors offer to register the product for you, but do not assume they did. Ask for the registration confirmation.

Registration must happen within the specified window, typically 60-90 days from installation. If you bought your system last year and are reading this today, it is too late for most brands. Check with your contractor to see if they registered it on your behalf.

What Voids an HVAC Warranty

Manufacturers look for reasons to deny warranty claims. Here are the actions that will void your coverage:

Improper installation. This is the number one reason warranty claims are denied. If the system was not installed by a licensed, insured HVAC contractor following manufacturer specifications, the warranty is void. DIY installation automatically voids every major brand's warranty. Using an unlicensed handyman also voids coverage.

Lack of maintenance. Manufacturers require proof of annual professional maintenance to honor warranty claims. If you file a claim and cannot show maintenance records, expect denial. Keep all receipts and service invoices. Some brands require maintenance to be performed by authorized dealers only.

Failure to register. As discussed above, missed registration drops coverage from 10 years to 5 years for most components.

Non-approved components. Using a non-approved thermostat, control board, or accessory can void the warranty on related components. If you install a third-party smart thermostat that causes a control board failure, the manufacturer will deny the claim.

Physical damage. Damage from floods, lightning strikes, power surges, animals, vandalism, or accidents is not covered. A surge protector for your HVAC system is a worthwhile investment for this reason.

Commercial use. Residential HVAC warranties do not cover equipment used in commercial applications. If you install a residential furnace in a rental property or business, the warranty may be voided. Check the terms for your specific brand.

Alterations or modifications. Any modification to the equipment, including changing refrigerant types, adding components, or altering the cabinet, voids the warranty.

Corrosion from environmental factors. Most warranties exclude corrosion damage from salt air, chemicals, or harsh environments. Coastal homeowners should check whether their brand covers corrosion-related failures.

Extended Warranties: Are They Worth It?

Extended warranties are available from both manufacturers and contractors. Here is how to evaluate whether they make sense for your situation:

Extended labor warranty: This covers the labor cost for warranty repairs after the standard 1-year contractor labor warranty expires. A 10-year extended labor warranty costs $400-$800. Given that a single warranty repair can cost $300-$800 in labor, this pays for itself after 1-2 service calls. If you plan to stay in your home for 10+ years, buy the extended labor warranty.

Extended parts warranty: Manufacturer parts warranties already cover 10 years on most components. Extended parts warranties that push coverage to 12-15 years add limited value because the system will likely be near replacement age by then. Skip extended parts coverage.

Home warranty: Third-party home warranty plans cover HVAC repairs for a service fee of $75-$150 per claim. These can be valuable for systems that are 8-15 years old and out of manufacturer warranty. For new systems with manufacturer coverage already in place, home warranties provide redundant coverage that rarely pays off.

How to Make a Warranty Claim

When your system fails and you believe it is covered under warranty, follow these steps:

  1. Call your installing contractor first. They have the relationship with the manufacturer and can process warranty claims most efficiently. If you cannot reach them, call a factory-authorized dealer for your brand.
  2. Have your model and serial numbers ready. These are on the data plate of the equipment. The contractor needs them to file the claim.
  3. Provide proof of registration. Have your warranty registration confirmation ready. If you did not register, ask the contractor to check whether the system was registered at the distributor level. Some distributors register units automatically.
  4. Provide maintenance records. Have receipts or invoices showing annual professional maintenance. Without these, the manufacturer can deny the claim regardless of coverage.
  5. Authorize the diagnostic fee. The contractor charges a diagnostic fee ($100-$250) to identify the problem. This fee is not covered by warranty even if the repair is covered. If the repair is covered, the contractor applies the diagnostic fee to the labor cost.
  6. Wait for parts approval. The contractor files the claim with the manufacturer. Parts approval typically takes 24-72 hours. Emergency parts may be available faster during peak seasons.
  7. Schedule the repair. Once parts arrive, the contractor schedules the repair. You pay for labor (unless you have an extended labor warranty). The manufacturer covers the parts.

Conclusion: Protect Your Investment

An HVAC warranty is valuable protection, but only if you understand what it covers and what you need to do to keep it valid. The three most important things you can do:

Register your product within 60 days. This single step preserves your 10-year parts coverage. Set a reminder on your phone the day the system is installed and register immediately.

Keep annual maintenance records. Save every service invoice. If your brand requires maintenance by authorized dealers, use only factory-authorized contractors. Documentation is your proof when filing a claim.

Consider an extended labor warranty. For $400-$800, you eliminate the risk of paying $300-$800 per repair for labor during years 2-10. For most homeowners, this is a worthwhile investment.
