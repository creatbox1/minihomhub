Federal Incentives USA 2026

The Inflation Reduction Act transformed heat pump incentives when it passed in 2022. Most provisions took effect in 2023 and run through 2032. Here is what is available in 2026.

25C Tax Credit — 30% of Total Installed Cost (up to $2,000)

This is the big one. The 25C Non-Business Energy Property Credit gives you 30% of the total installed cost of a qualifying heat pump, up to a maximum of $2,000 per year. It applies per dwelling — if you install heat pumps in a primary home and a vacation home, you can claim it for both.

Key details:

  • Annual cap: $2,000 per year (not per lifetime — you can claim again in future years for additional installations).
  • Effective dates: January 1, 2023 through December 31, 2032.
  • No income limit: Every taxpayer qualifies regardless of income.
  • Non-refundable: The credit reduces your tax bill to zero but will not generate a refund for the excess. If you owe $1,500 in taxes and claim a $2,000 credit, you get $1,500.
  • Carryover: Unused credit amounts do not carry forward to future tax years — another reason to plan your installation year carefully.
  • Qualifying equipment: Any ENERGY STAR-certified heat pump meeting minimum SEER2 (≥15) and HSPF2 (≥7.5) requirements. Cold-climate heat pumps qualify under the same credit.

High-Efficiency Electric Home Rebate Program (HEEHR) — Not Yet Active

Originally part of the Inflation Reduction Act, the HEEHR program promised point-of-sale rebates of up to $8,000 for low-to-moderate-income households. As of mid-2026, implementation varies wildly by state. Some states have launched (New York, California, Maine), while others are still setting up their programs. Check the DOE website for your state's status.

Federal Incentives Canada 2026

Canada offers three main federal streams for heat pump incentives. The amounts are in Canadian dollars and stack with provincial programs.

Canada Greener Homes Grant (CGHG)

The flagship federal program provides up to $5,000 CAD for qualifying heat pump installations. Key requirements:

  • Pre- and post-retrofit EnerGuide evaluations by a licensed energy advisor (costs up to $600, partially covered by the grant).
  • Heat pump must meet minimum efficiency: HSPF2 ≥ 10 for cold-climate units, or equivalent.
  • Grant covers up to $5,000 of the heat pump cost, including installation and necessary electrical upgrades.
  • Must be installed in a primary residence — rental properties and new construction do not qualify.
  • Application must be approved before installation begins. Retroactive applications are not accepted.

Oil to Heat Pump Affordability (OHPA) Program

Launched in 2024 and expanded in 2025-2026, OHPA provides up to $15,000 CAD upfront — not a rebate, an upfront grant — to low-to-median-income households currently heating with oil who switch to a cold-climate heat pump. The grant covers:

  • Heat pump equipment and installation (up to $10,000).
  • Required electrical panel upgrades (up to $2,500).
  • Oil tank removal (up to $2,500).
  • Can be stacked with the Greener Homes Grant for total federal support of up to $20,000.

Eligibility: Household income at or below the median total income for your area. The program pays the contractor directly — you do not pay upfront and wait for reimbursement. This is significant because it removes the cash-flow barrier that stops many families from switching off oil.

Canada Greener Homes Loan

For homeowners who do not qualify for OHPA or want additional funding, the Greener Homes Loan offers up to $40,000 in zero-interest financing over 10 years. The loan covers heat pumps, solar panels, windows, insulation, and other retrofits. No payments are required for the first 12 months. This is not a grant — it must be repaid — but zero interest over a decade makes it highly attractive.

Utility & Manufacturer Rebates

Do not stop at federal and state/provincial programs. Utility companies and manufacturers offer additional rebates that are often overlooked.

Utility Company Rebates

Many electric utilities offer rebates for heat pumps because they reduce peak demand in summer and winter. Typical amounts: $300 to $1,200. Some notable programs:

  • Xcel Energy (MN, CO, MI, TX): $1,000-$1,500 for cold-climate heat pumps.
  • Puget Sound Energy (WA): $1,200 for qualifying heat pumps.
  • National Grid (NY, RI, MA): $750-$1,500 depending on territory.
  • Eversource (CT, MA, NH): $500-$1,000 for heat pumps.
  • Duke Energy (OH, NC, SC, FL): $400-$800 depending on state.

Manufacturer Rebates

HVAC manufacturers offer seasonal promotions — typically $200 to $1,000 off when purchased through a participating dealer. These are separate from the dealer's own pricing. Ask your contractor about current manufacturer rebates from Mitsubishi, Daikin, Carrier, Trane, LG, and others. These stack with everything else.

How to Stack Incentives — The Real Key to Maximum Savings

Stacking — combining multiple incentives on a single installation — is where the real savings happen. Here is how to do it in the right order:

Layer 1: Federal US Tax Credit (30%, up to $2,000) or Federal Canada Greener Homes ($5,000)
Start with the biggest program. In the US, the 30% tax credit is calculated on total installed cost before any other rebates are subtracted. This is critical: a $12,000 system gets a $3,600 tax credit even if you receive a $3,000 state rebate. In Canada, the Greener Homes Grant requires pre-approval before installation.

Layer 2: State or Provincial Rebate
Apply for your state or provincial rebate next. Most programs allow stacking with the federal credit. Some require the federal credit to be factored in — check the fine print. Example: Mass Save ($10,000) + Federal ($2,000) = up to $12,000 off a $15,000 installation.

Layer 3: Utility Rebate
Utility rebates almost always stack with everything. They are typically smaller ($300-$1,500) but every dollar counts. Apply after confirming state/provincial approval.

Layer 4: Manufacturer Rebate
This is usually handled by your contractor at the point of sale. Ask about current promotions before signing.

Eligibility, Qualified Equipment & Requirements

Before you apply, confirm your system and situation meet the requirements. Here are the most common eligibility rules across programs:

Equipment Requirements

  • ENERGY STAR certification: Required for the federal US tax credit and most state/utility programs. The heat pump must have the ENERGY STAR label.
  • Minimum efficiency: SEER2 ≥ 15, HSPF2 ≥ 7.5 for standard. Cold-climate programs require HSPF2 ≥ 10 and specific COP thresholds.
  • New equipment only: Used, refurbished, or salvaged heat pumps do not qualify for any major incentive program.
  • Professional installation: DIY installations are excluded from almost every rebate program. The contractor must be licensed and insured.

Homeowner Requirements

  • Primary residence: Most programs require the heat pump to be installed in your primary residence. Second homes and rental properties are often excluded (check your specific program).
  • Existing home: New construction typically does not qualify for retrofit incentive programs. The home must exist before installation.
  • Tax liability (US credit): You must have enough federal tax liability to use the credit. If your total tax bill is less than $2,000, you will not receive the full credit.

Step-by-Step Application Guide

Here is the exact process I guide my audit clients through. Follow these steps in order and you will maximize your rebates while minimizing stress.

  1. Get a home energy audit. Many programs require one. Even if not required, an audit identifies insulation gaps and duct leaks that affect heat pump sizing. Cost: $300-$600 (often partially covered by the rebate program).
  2. Research available incentives. Use the tables above as a starting point, then confirm current amounts on the program websites. Note application deadlines and funding caps.
  3. Choose qualifying equipment. Buy from the ENERGY STAR certified list. Confirm the specific model number meets your target program's requirements before signing any contract.
  4. Get three quotes from licensed contractors. Ask each contractor if they participate in your target rebate programs. Some contractors are not registered with certain programs. Request line-item pricing.
  5. Apply for pre-approval (if required). The Canada Greener Homes Grant and several US state programs require pre-approval before installation. Submit your energy audit and contractor quote. Wait for approval before proceeding.
  6. Schedule installation. Keep all documentation: contract, model numbers, serial numbers, receipts, and contractor license numbers.
  7. Submit post-installation paperwork. Most programs require final inspection or post-retrofit energy audit confirmation. Submit within the specified window (usually 30-90 days after installation).
  8. Claim federal tax credit. File IRS Form 5695 with your next tax return. Keep the manufacturer's certification statement and your receipts.
  9. Track your rebate payments. Follow up at 4-week intervals if you have not received payment within the estimated processing time.

Common Pitfalls to Avoid

After watching hundreds of homeowners navigate this process, here are the mistakes I see most often:

Pitfall 1: Installing before getting pre-approval. The Canada Greener Homes Grant and several state programs reject applications submitted after installation begins. Always confirm pre-approval requirements before signing a contract.

Pitfall 2: Buying equipment that does not qualify. Not every heat pump qualifies. Check the specific model number against your target program's qualified products list. A contractor telling you "it should qualify" is not good enough — get written confirmation.

Pitfall 3: Assuming the contractor handles the paperwork. Some contractors submit rebate applications on your behalf. Many do not. Confirm in writing who is responsible for each application. I recommend handling the federal tax credit yourself — it is simple and ensures accuracy.

Pitfall 4: Missing the federal credit calculation. The 30% tax credit is calculated on total installed cost before other rebates. But some state programs require you to subtract the federal credit from the cost before calculating their rebate. Check the calculation order for each program.

Pitfall 5: Not having enough tax liability. The federal credit is non-refundable. If you owe $1,000 in taxes, you only get $1,000 of the credit. Retirees, part-time workers, and low-income households may not benefit fully. Consider splitting the installation across two tax years if you expect higher income next year.

Pitfall 6: Forgetting to save all documentation. Rebate programs audit applications. Keep your contract, model numbers, serial numbers, receipts, manufacturer certification, energy audit reports, and all correspondence. Store digital copies in at least two places.

Pitfall 7: Ignoring manufacturer rebates. These are free money that takes 30 seconds to claim. Ask your contractor what promotions are running before you sign.
