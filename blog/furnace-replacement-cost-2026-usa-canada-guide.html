Furnace Replacement Cost by Fuel Type

Gas Furnace Replacement Costs

Natural gas furnaces dominate the North American market, heating roughly 50% of US homes and 60% of Canadian homes. They offer the lowest operating cost of any fuel source in most regions. Here is how gas furnace pricing breaks down by AFUE rating:

80% AFUE Single-Stage ($4,500-$6,500): These basic furnaces operate at full capacity whenever the thermostat calls for heat. They are simple, reliable, and the least expensive option. The 80% rating means 80% of the fuel converts to heat, while 20% goes up the flue. These are no longer allowed in some northern states due to efficiency standards, but remain popular in milder climates and as budget replacements.

80% AFUE Two-Stage ($5,500-$8,000): Two-stage furnaces run at low capacity most of the time, kicking into high gear only when temperatures drop. They provide more even temperatures, better comfort, and slightly higher efficiency than single-stage units. The two-stage operation also reduces temperature swings and improves air filtration by running longer cycles.

95-96% AFUE Two-Stage ($6,500-$9,500): These condensing furnaces capture heat that would otherwise escape up the flue, extracting extra BTUs from combustion gases. The 95%+ efficiency rating means nearly all your fuel dollar goes toward heating your home. These furnaces require PVC venting instead of metal chimney flues, which adds some cost but allows installation in homes without existing chimneys. The federal minimum for new installations in northern states is 90% AFUE as of 2026, making this the entry-level choice for homeowners in cold climates.

97-98% AFUE Modulating ($8,500-$14,000): These top-tier furnaces adjust heat output continuously between roughly 35% and 100% of capacity. A modulating furnace matches heat output precisely to your home's heat loss, running at low speeds for extended periods. This delivers the most consistent temperatures, the quietest operation, and the highest efficiency available. The premium over a 96% two-stage furnace runs $2,000-$4,000, which takes about 5-8 years to recover through energy savings.

Electric Furnace Replacement Costs

Electric furnaces cost less to buy and install than gas furnaces, ranging from $3,000 to $6,500 for a complete installation. They operate at 100% efficiency because all electricity converts to heat. However, electricity costs 2-3 times more per BTU than natural gas in most markets, making electric furnaces significantly more expensive to run.

Electric furnaces make sense in three situations: homes without natural gas service, mild climates where the furnace runs only occasionally, and areas with very low electricity rates (under 8 cents per kWh). In most other cases, a heat pump provides better value than a standard electric furnace.

Oil Furnace Replacement Costs

Oil furnaces remain common in the Northeast US and parts of Atlantic Canada, where natural gas infrastructure is limited. Replacement costs range from $5,000 to $9,000 installed. Oil furnaces are more complex than gas furnaces, requiring oil tanks, burner maintenance, and annual cleaning. Oil prices fluctuate more than natural gas, making long-term operating costs harder to predict.

What Is Included in the Price?

When a contractor quotes you a furnace replacement price, here is exactly what that covers:

Removal and Disposal: The contractor disconnects, removes, and hauls away your old furnace. Proper disposal of the old unit, including any hazardous components, is included.

New Furnace Equipment: The furnace unit itself, which accounts for roughly 40-50% of the total price. The brand, AFUE rating, and blower type determine the equipment cost.

Labor and Installation: The largest cost component at 30-40% of the total. This includes positioning the new furnace, connecting gas and electrical lines, installing venting, setting up the thermostat, and testing the system.

Venting Materials: Condensing furnaces require PVC venting to the outdoors. For non-condensing furnaces, the contractor may reuse your existing chimney or install new metal flue piping. Venting materials typically add $200-$600 to the total.

Permits and Inspections: Most municipalities require permits for gas furnace replacement. Permit fees range from $100 to $500 and should be included in your quote. The contractor schedules and pays for the inspection.

Thermostat: A basic programmable thermostat is usually included. Upgrading to a smart thermostat costs $100-$300 extra.

System Startup and Commissioning: The contractor tests gas pressure, verifies combustion efficiency, measures temperature rise, checks safety controls, and confirms proper airflow before leaving.

What Is Not Included

These extras often come up during furnace replacement and are typically not included in the base quote:

  • Ductwork modifications: $500-$3,000. If your ducts are undersized, leaky, or not compatible with the new furnace's airflow.
  • Gas line upgrades: $200-$800. If the existing gas line is undersized for the new furnace's BTU rating.
  • Electrical panel upgrade: $1,500-$3,000. Needed if your service panel cannot handle the additional load.
  • Humidifier or air purifier: $400-$1,500. Add-ons that integrate with the new system.
  • Media cabinet or filter upgrade: $200-$600. For larger or more efficient filter systems.
  • Extended labor warranty: $300-$800. Covers labor costs beyond the standard 1-year contractor warranty.

Factors That Affect Furnace Replacement Cost

AFUE Efficiency Rating

AFUE (Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency) measures the percentage of fuel converted to heat. An 80% AFUE furnace wastes 20% of your fuel, while a 96% AFUE furnace wastes only 4%. The higher the AFUE, the higher the equipment cost, but the lower your monthly heating bills. Here is how the tiers compare for a typical 2,500-square-foot home in a northern climate:

Home Size and Heating Load

Furnace size is measured in BTUs (British Thermal Units), with typical residential furnaces ranging from 40,000 to 140,000 BTUs. A properly sized furnace performs a heat loss calculation based on your home's square footage, insulation levels, window quality, and climate. Here are general guidelines:

  • 1,000-1,500 sq ft: 40,000-60,000 BTUs. Installed cost $5,000-$7,500.
  • 1,500-2,500 sq ft: 60,000-80,000 BTUs. Installed cost $6,000-$9,000.
  • 2,500-3,500 sq ft: 80,000-110,000 BTUs. Installed cost $7,000-$11,000.
  • 3,500-4,500 sq ft: 110,000-140,000 BTUs. Installed cost $8,500-$14,000.

An oversized furnace cycles on and off frequently, creating temperature swings and wasting energy. An undersized furnace runs constantly without reaching your desired temperature on the coldest days. Proper sizing through a Manual J calculation ensures optimal performance and efficiency.

How to Reduce Furnace Replacement Costs

Time Your Purchase for Off-Season

Furnace demand peaks from October through February. Contractors charge premium rates during heating season because emergency calls take priority. Schedule your replacement between March and September for off-season savings of 10-20%. Some contractors offer winter pricing if you book early in the fall.

Get at Least Three Quotes

Furnace pricing varies dramatically between contractors. Get detailed quotes from at least three licensed, insured contractors. Each quote should specify the furnace make and model, AFUE rating, warranty terms, and exactly what is included. Do not automatically choose the lowest price. Compare the total value including equipment quality and warranty coverage.

Claim Available Rebates

Many utility companies and state programs offer rebates for high-efficiency furnace replacements. The Inflation Reduction Act provides federal tax credits of up to $2,000 for qualifying heat pump systems. While gas furnaces do not qualify for the full credit, some high-efficiency gas furnaces paired with electric heat pumps may qualify for partial incentives. Check with your utility company and on the DSIRE database for available programs in your area.

Bundle with AC Replacement

If your air conditioner is also nearing the end of its life, replacing both systems together saves $500-$1,500 on combined labor. Many contractors offer package pricing for complete HVAC system replacements. The combined efficiency of a matched furnace and AC system also delivers maximum energy savings.

Consider a Heat Pump

In moderate climates, a heat pump can replace both your furnace and air conditioner. Heat pumps provide efficient heating down to about 25 degrees Fahrenheit and cost roughly the same as a furnace-plus-AC combination. They qualify for up to $2,000 in federal tax credits and significantly lower annual energy costs in regions with mild winters.

Conclusion

A furnace replacement in 2026 is a significant investment, but one that pays returns every month through lower energy bills. The sweet spot for most homeowners is a 96% AFUE two-stage gas furnace from a reputable brand, installed by a qualified contractor. This combination delivers the best balance of upfront cost, operating efficiency, and long-term reliability.

Start by getting Manual J load calculations from at least three contractors. Compare their equipment and pricing side by side. Look into rebates and incentives available in your area. And if your AC is also getting old, consider replacing both systems together for maximum savings.
