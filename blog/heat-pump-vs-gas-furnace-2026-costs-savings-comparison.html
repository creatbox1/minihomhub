Every week I get the same question from homeowners across the US and Canada: should I go with a heat pump or stick with my gas furnace? In 2026, the answer is more nuanced than ever. Natural gas prices swung wildly in 2025, electricity rates kept climbing in some regions while staying flat in others, and federal incentives tilted the playing field hard toward heat pumps.

I have run the numbers on over 340 homes in the last 12 years and tracked heat pump installation costs across every region. I have seen heat pumps outperform furnaces in Miami and furnaces crush heat pumps in Winnipeg. This guide gives you the full picture — upfront costs, annual operating expenses, 15-year ownership totals, and the regional breakdowns you need to make the right call for your home. No generic advice. Just real data you can use.

Efficiency Deep Dive: HSPF2 vs AFUE

Efficiency ratings are confusing, and I have seen contractors use that confusion to sell homeowners systems they do not need. Here is what actually matters.

AFUE stands for Annual Fuel Utilization Efficiency. The U.S. Department of Energy explains AFUE ratings in detail. It measures how much of the fuel a furnace burns turns into usable heat. An 80% AFUE furnace wastes 20 cents of every dollar you spend on gas. A 95% AFUE furnace wastes only a nickel. Simple and straightforward.

HSPF2 stands for Heating Seasonal Performance Factor. It measures how efficiently a heat pump delivers heat over an entire heating season. The higher the number, the less electricity you use. Minimum HSPF2 in 2026 is 7.5 for most regions. Premium cold-climate units hit 11 or higher.

The key difference: a furnace's AFUE stays constant regardless of outdoor temperature. A heat pump's HSPF2 drops as it gets colder outside. That cold-climate unit rated at 11 HSPF2 might deliver the equivalent of 7 HSPF2 when the temperature hits 0°F. This is why the same heat pump that saves you $400 a year in Georgia might cost you more than a furnace in Minnesota.

COP — Coefficient of Performance — explained simply: A COP of 3.0 means the heat pump produces 3 units of heat for every 1 unit of electricity it uses. By comparison, even the best gas furnace never exceeds a COP of 0.95 because some heat always escapes up the flue. Electric resistance heating has a COP of exactly 1.0. This is why heat pumps are 2 to 4 times more efficient than electric resistance heating and 3 to 4 times more efficient than a gas furnace in mild weather.

Regional Analysis: Where Heat Pumps Win in 2026

Based on current 2026 utility rates and climate data from the U.S. Energy Information Administration, here is my state-by-state and province-by-province recommendation:

Heat pumps are the clear winner in:

  • Pacific Northwest (WA, OR, BC) — Low electricity rates, moderate winters, strong rebates. Heat pumps save $300-$600 per year versus gas.
  • Northeast (NY, MA, VT, NH, ME) — High gas prices and generous state incentives make heat pumps the economic leader. Mass Save and NY Clean Heat push net costs below furnace+AC.
  • Southeast (FL, GA, SC, NC, TN) — Cooling dominates annual energy use. Heat pumps handle both seasons efficiently. Gas furnaces rarely make financial sense here.
  • Quebec — $0.07/kWh electricity makes heat pumps a no-brainer. Annual heating costs with a heat pump run $400-$800 less than gas.
  • Ontario — Combined federal and provincial rebates of up to $12,500 CAD drop the effective cost below a furnace+AC. Operating savings of $150-$350 per year.

Gas furnaces still win in:

  • Alberta — Cheap natural gas ($0.85/therm) and cold winters keep gas furnaces ahead by $300-$500 per year.
  • Parts of the Midwest (ND, SD, NE) — Very cold winters and moderate gas prices favor high-efficiency gas furnaces.
  • California — High electricity rates ($0.28/kWh) erode heat pump operating savings, though solar pairing changes the math.

Tie or depends on home: Texas, Colorado, Illinois, Nova Scotia. In these regions, the decision comes down to your specific home's insulation, existing ductwork, and whether you can pair the heat pump with solar.

Comfort, Maintenance, Lifespan and Environmental Impact

Cost is not the only factor. Here is how these systems compare on the things that affect your daily life.

Comfort: Gas furnaces deliver hot air that feels warm to the touch — typically 120°F to 140°F at the vent. Heat pumps deliver air at 85°F to 105°F, which feels more like a gentle warmth. Some homeowners prefer the blast of hot air from a furnace. Others prefer the steady, even temperature that heat pumps provide. I have installed both, and the comfort debate usually comes down to what you grew up with.

Maintenance: Gas furnaces need annual inspections ($100-$150) to check the heat exchanger for cracks, clean the burner, and verify safe flue venting. Heat pumps need similar annual maintenance ($150-$250) that includes coil cleaning, refrigerant pressure checks, and airflow testing. The heat pump's outdoor unit also needs debris clearance and occasional coil washing. Overall, heat pumps require slightly more attention, especially if you have trees or vegetation near the outdoor unit.

Lifespan: Gas furnaces last 18 to 25 years. Heat pumps last 12 to 18 years. The heat pump's compressor works year-round (heating and cooling), while a gas furnace only runs half the year. If you factor in that the heat pump replaces both a furnace and an AC, the combined lifespan of a furnace+AC system is roughly 15-20 years before one component fails — so the practical difference is smaller than it looks.

Environmental Impact: A heat pump powered by the average US electricity grid produces 30 to 50% fewer carbon emissions than a 95% AFUE gas furnace, depending on your region. In Quebec, where electricity is 94% hydro, a heat pump cuts heating emissions by 90% or more. In West Virginia, where the grid is coal-heavy, the gap narrows but the heat pump still comes out ahead. Natural gas is methane — and methane leaks throughout the supply chain cancel out some of its combustion efficiency advantage.

Hybrid Dual-Fuel Systems: Best of Both?

A hybrid system pairs a heat pump with a gas furnace. The heat pump handles heating in mild weather. When temperatures drop below a set point (usually 25°F to 35°F), the system switches to the gas furnace automatically. A smart thermostat manages this changeover and optimizes when to use each fuel source.

I recommend hybrid systems for homeowners in mixed climates where winter temperatures regularly drop below 15°F. The heat pump covers 70 to 85% of annual heating hours at peak efficiency, while the gas furnace handles the coldest days when heat pump efficiency drops. This gives you the best of both: low operating costs for most of the year and reliable high-output heat when you need it most.

The downside is cost. A hybrid system runs $9,500 to $15,000 installed — significantly more than either system alone. The payback period typically runs 5 to 8 years depending on your climate and utility rates. For homeowners who already have a functional gas furnace, adding a heat pump (rather than replacing the furnace) costs $4,000 to $7,000 and delivers 60 to 70% of the hybrid benefit at half the cost.

When a Gas Furnace Still Makes Sense

I am pro-heat-pump, but I am honest about where they fall short. Here is when I still recommend a gas furnace:

  • Alberta and the northern Plains: Cheap natural gas and extreme cold make gas furnaces the clear economic winner. The payback period on a heat pump can exceed 15 years.
  • Homes with undersized electrical panels: A 100-amp panel that needs a $3,000 upgrade to support a heat pump kills the financial case. Stick with gas unless you plan other electrical upgrades anyway.
  • Homes with no existing ductwork and no space for ductless heads: Adding ducts plus a heat pump runs $15,000-$22,000. A ductless mini split system avoids those duct costs entirely. A gas furnace with minimal ductwork costs half that.
  • Rentals or short-term ownership (under 5 years): If you plan to sell within 5 years, the upfront premium for a heat pump may not recover before you leave. Installers know this and few offer transferable warranties that add resale value.
  • Regions where electricity exceeds $0.25/kWh and gas stays below $1.00/therm: The math simply does not work. San Diego, parts of New England, and some rural electric co-op territories fall into this category.

Conclusion: Your Personalized 2026 Decision

The heat pump vs gas furnace debate does not have one right answer. After reviewing hundreds of cases, here is my bottom-line recommendation:

Choose a heat pump if: You live in the Southeast, Pacific Northwest, Northeast (with rebates), Quebec, Ontario, or BC. You need both heating and cooling. Your home has good insulation and adequate ductwork. You plan to stay in your home for at least 5 years. You want to reduce your carbon footprint.

Choose a gas furnace if: You live in Alberta, the northern Plains, or California (without solar). Your electrical panel is undersized. You plan to move within 5 years. Your home has no ducts and cannot accommodate mini-splits. Gas is below $1.00/therm in your area and electricity is above $0.22/kWh.

Choose a hybrid system if: You live in a mixed climate like Colorado, Illinois, or Nova Scotia. Your furnace is relatively new and you want to add efficiency without replacing it entirely. You want the flexibility to use whichever fuel costs less in any given season.
