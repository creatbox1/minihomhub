Federal USA HVAC Tax Credits 2026

The Inflation Reduction Act of 2022 overhauled the federal energy efficiency tax credits, making them more accessible and removing the lifetime dollar limits that previously restricted homeowners. These credits are officially called the Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (IRC 25C). Here is what is available in 2026:

Key details about the federal credits that many homeowners miss. For a complete breakdown of heat pump-specific incentives, see our heat pump rebates and incentives guide.

No lifetime dollar limit. Under the old rules, homeowners could only claim a total of $500 in lifetime HVAC tax credits. The Inflation Reduction Act removed all lifetime caps. You can claim the full credit amount each time you install qualifying equipment, year after year.

No annual cap on claims. You can claim multiple credits in the same tax year. For example, install a heat pump ($2,000 credit) and central AC ($600 credit) in the same year and claim both. The total 30% calculation applies to each qualifying product separately.

No income limits. Unlike some other Inflation Reduction Act programs, the 25C tax credit has no income restrictions. Any homeowner with federal tax liability can claim it, regardless of income level.

Existing homes only. The credit applies only to improvements made to your existing primary residence. New construction does not qualify. Second homes and rental properties also do not qualify for the credit. How to Stack Incentives for Maximum Savings

The most money comes from combining multiple incentives. Here is exactly how stacking works for a typical heat pump installation:

Scenario: Installing a qualifying 18 SEER2 heat pump for $8,500

  • Federal 25C tax credit: 30% of $8,500 = $2,000 (capped at $2,000)
  • State rebate (e.g., NY Clean Heat): $4,000
  • Utility rebate: $500
  • Total incentives: $6,500
  • Net cost after all incentives: $2,000

The rules for stacking incentives are straightforward:

Federal + State: You can claim the federal tax credit regardless of state rebates received. The IRS does not reduce your federal credit because you also received a state rebate. However, you cannot claim the federal credit on the portion of the cost already covered by a state rebate. If a state rebate covers $1,000 of your $8,500 project, you claim the federal 30% on the remaining $7,500.

Federal + Utility: Same rules apply. Utility rebates are treated as a reduction in cost, not as taxable income. Claim the federal credit on the net cost after the utility rebate.

State + Utility: Most states allow combining their rebates with utility rebates. Some utility programs actually require you to apply for state rebates first. Check the order of operations with both programs before applying.

Canada stacking: Provincial rebates and the Canada Greener Homes Loan can be combined. The loan is interest-free financing, not a grant, so it does not affect your qualification for other rebates. Some municipalities offer additional top-up rebates for heat pump installations.

Step-by-Step Guide to Claiming HVAC Tax Credits

Claiming your HVAC tax credit is straightforward if you follow these steps. Missing one can cost you the entire credit, so pay attention to the details.

Step 1: Verify equipment eligibility before you buy. Not all high-efficiency HVAC equipment qualifies for the federal tax credit. Before signing the contract, confirm that the specific model you are buying is on the ENERGY STAR qualified products list or has a manufacturer certification statement confirming it meets the CEE highest tier requirements. Your contractor should provide this documentation. Do not rely on sales brochures or verbal claims.

Step 2: Keep all documentation. You need three pieces of documentation: the manufacturer's ENERGY STAR certification statement, the installation contract showing the installed cost, and the paid invoice or receipt showing the date of installation. Keep these with your tax records for at least three years in case of an IRS audit.

Step 3: Complete IRS Form 5695. Download IRS Form 5695 (Residential Energy Credits) for the 2026 tax year. Part II covers the Residential Energy Efficient Property Credit for geothermal heat pumps. Part I covers the Nonbusiness Energy Property Credit for all other HVAC equipment. Enter the qualified product costs and calculate the credit. The form includes instructions for each line.

Step 4: File with your tax return. Attach Form 5695 to your 2026 federal tax return. The credit reduces your tax liability dollar for dollar. If the credit exceeds your tax liability, unused amounts do not carry forward to future years.

Step 5: For state rebates, apply before installation. Some state rebate programs require pre-approval. Others require you to file after installation with receipts and contractor information. Check the specific requirements for your state program. Utility rebates typically require a post-installation application within 90 days of installation. Some programs have limited funding that runs out mid-year.

Step 6: For Canadian programs, check timing. Provincial programs have varying application windows and funding availability. CleanBC programs in British Columbia require pre-approval before installation. Ontario's Home Renovation Savings program allows post-installation applications but requires proof that the equipment meets efficiency standards. Alberta's municipal programs vary by city. Always confirm current program status before scheduling installation.

HVAC Tax Credit Eligibility Checklist

Before you commit to an HVAC purchase, run through this checklist to ensure you qualify for the maximum incentives:

  • The equipment is installed at your existing primary residence (not new construction)
  • The unit meets the specific efficiency requirements for the credit you want (SEER2, HSPF2, AFUE thresholds)
  • You have the manufacturer's ENERGY STAR certification statement for the exact model you are buying
  • The installation is completed before December 31, 2026
  • You have sufficient federal tax liability to use the full credit amount
  • For state rebates: you meet any income limits and application deadlines
  • For utility rebates: the program still has available funding
  • For Canadian homeowners: you have pre-approval if required by your provincial program
